\section*{Introduction}

Type~1 diabetes (T1D) affects an estimated nine million people worldwide and its
incidence is rising in every region~\cite{idf2021}. People living with T1D must
continually balance insulin, food, and activity to keep blood glucose within a
narrow target range; sustained excursions above or below that range drive both
the acute dangers of hypoglycaemia and the long-term microvascular and
cardiovascular complications of hyperglycaemia. Continuous glucose monitoring
(CGM), which reports interstitial glucose every five minutes, is now the
standard of care and the basis on which clinical outcomes such as time in range
are defined~\cite{battelino2019tir}. The same dense data stream makes
short-horizon glucose \emph{forecasting} clinically valuable: a reliable
prediction of where glucose will be in 30--60 minutes can trigger preemptive
alerts, inform bolus and snack decisions, and close the loop in automated
insulin-delivery systems~\cite{vettoretti2020advanced,bekiari2018ap}. Accurate
forecasting is therefore not an academic exercise but a component of
safety-critical decision support.

Short-horizon CGM forecasting has progressed from autoregressive and classical
machine-learning models to deep sequence models. Recurrent architectures with
calibrated uncertainty established strong baselines on benchmark
cohorts~\cite{martinsson2020}, and standardised datasets and challenges shaped
much of the early evaluation~\cite{marling2020ohio}. The
transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention} and its long-horizon time-series
variants~\cite{zhou2021informer,wu2021autoformer} have since been adapted to
glucose, with personalised, uncertainty-aware transformer forecasters reporting
state-of-the-art accuracy~\cite{sergazinov2023gluformer}, and the curation of
multiple public CGM datasets into shared prediction
benchmarks~\cite{sergazinov2024glucobench} has exposed how strongly measured
performance depends on the cohort and the evaluation protocol. A clinically
important caveat runs through this literature: at short horizons glucose is
highly autocorrelated, so a naive persistence predictor is a deceptively strong
baseline, and apparent differences between sophisticated models can be small
once that floor is accounted for. Crucially, almost all of this work assumes
centrally pooled data and a single training distribution, leaving open how such
models behave when training must be distributed across heterogeneous,
privacy-siloed cohorts.

That assumption is the field's central practical obstacle. Deep forecasters are
data-hungry, and their accuracy and---more importantly---their ability to
generalise to new patients depend on training over large, diverse populations.
Yet CGM traces are sensitive personal health records, fragmented across
hospitals, clinical trials, device manufacturers, and national registries, and
bound by data-protection regimes such as the GDPR and HIPAA that make pooling
raw records across institutions, let alone across borders, legally and ethically
fraught. The data that models need (large, heterogeneous corpora) and the data
that exist (many small, governed silos) are mismatched. Federated learning (FL)
was introduced to resolve exactly this tension~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}:
participating sites train on their own data and exchange only model updates, so a
shared model can be learned without any raw record leaving its institution. FL
has been embraced as a foundational technology for collaborative medical machine
learning~\cite{rieke2020future,sheller2020federated,kaissis2020secure}.
Statistical heterogeneity across sites is its central difficulty: plain
federated averaging degrades when client distributions diverge, motivating
proximal regularisation~\cite{li2020fedprox} and control-variate variance
reduction~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} to stabilise aggregation. This body of
work, however, is evaluated almost entirely in simulation, where ``clients'' are
partitions of one dataset on a single machine.

\textbf{This exposes a first gap: federated glucose models are almost never
deployed, only simulated.} Simulation elides the problems that actually arise
when sites are real, physically separate, and internationally distributed:
establishing connectivity across institutional firewalls without exposing public
ports; ensuring that clients with mismatched hardware, operating systems, and
numerical-library versions produce mutually compatible updates; agreeing on a
byte-identical model initialisation across heterogeneous machines; and
synchronising rounds when participants come and go. These are not incidental
engineering details---they determine whether a federation can run at all, and
they constrain which algorithms are admissible. The gap between a simulated
federation and a deployed one is the gap between a result and a usable system.

Real CGM cohorts also differ in device generation, demographics, treatment
protocols, and glycaemic regime, and these differences widen across countries. A
federated model must therefore cope with substantial domain shift: it should
generalise to patients, and even whole cohorts, it never trained on (domain
generalisation), and/or specialise to each site (personalisation). Domain
generalisation seeks models that transfer to unseen
distributions~\cite{zhou2022dgsurvey}, and meta-learning for domain
generalisation (MLDG) simulates train/test domain shift within each update so the
model learns to generalise rather than memorise~\cite{li2018mldg}. Bringing these
ideas into federation is an active area surveyed by Li et
al.~\cite{li2024fedDGsurvey}, and the dominant families map onto the methods we
compare. Meta-learning approaches adapt the model-agnostic meta-learning
objective to federation, for communication-efficient
convergence~\cite{chen2018fedmeta} or for models that personalise to a new client
in a few gradient steps~\cite{fallah2020perfedavg}. Personalisation approaches
keep a per-client model: APFL learns an adaptive convex mixture of the global and
a local model~\cite{deng2020apfl}, while Ditto regularises each client's personal
model toward the global one with a tunable proximal
strength~\cite{li2021ditto}. Representation-alignment approaches reduce
cross-client shift in feature space, via model-contrastive
objectives~\cite{li2021moon}, federated contrastive learning for multi-centre
medical imaging~\cite{fedcl2023}, or adversarial domain alignment for federated
generalisation~\cite{flda2023,fedadv2022}.

\textbf{This exposes a second gap: the methods that handle cross-site
heterogeneity are rarely compared head-to-head, and their evaluation hides
confounds.} These methods are seldom benchmarked on a common glucose-forecasting
task, and evaluations rarely separate the two regimes that matter
clinically---accuracy on patients from training cohorts (held-in) versus accuracy
on entirely unseen cohorts (out of distribution, OOD). We find these regimes can
disagree, and that strong OOD numbers can arise from a covariate-match
artifact, in which a model trained on a cohort whose glucose distribution happens
to coincide with the held-out one generalises well for reasons unrelated to its
training algorithm---an effect easily mistaken for genuine methodological skill.
We also find that the \emph{type} of personalisation matters: an adaptive
global/local mixture can collapse toward the global model on the most
heterogeneous cohort, whereas a structural split (a shared encoder with a
client-specific decoder) recovers it.

Finally, a smaller body of work treats FL as a deployed system rather than an
algorithm, addressing client--server communication, fault tolerance, and the
heterogeneity of real participating devices. Production frameworks exist, but they
carry substantial dependencies and assumptions that can be ill-suited to a small
clinical consortium that simply wants a handful of sites, one cohort each, to
train together. To our knowledge, an end-to-end, networked, cross-site federation
for CGM forecasting---validated on physically separate machines and designed for
multi-country operation---has not previously been reported.

This paper addresses both gaps with two co-equal contributions. First, we build,
from scratch, a real cross-site FL system for CGM forecasting and validate it on
physically separate machines rather than in a simulator. It comprises a stateless
aggregation server that holds the global model but no data, and lightweight
clients that each wrap one institution's cohort; communication uses only the
standard library over HTTP, so a participating site can be handed a single
bundle, install one requirements file, and join with one command. The server
mints and broadcasts the initial weights so that every client begins
byte-identical regardless of hardware, and the same protocol carries FedAvg,
FedProx, and meta-learned (MLDG) training with the choice made entirely
client-side. We report an end-to-end run across two physically distinct machines
connected over the public internet through a reverse SSH tunnel, and describe the
design that extends this to a geographically distributed, multi-country
deployment. Second, we contribute a controlled domain-generalisation benchmark of
federated strategies for transformer-based glucose forecasting. Using a common
encoder--decoder transformer and four real T1D cohorts as federated clients, plus
held-out cohorts as OOD test sites, we compare single-cohort local training,
FedAvg, FedProx, MLDG, and two personalised-FL methods (APFL and Ditto) under a
multi-seed protocol. Our analysis shows that meta-learned federation (MLDG) is
generally the strongest method both held-in and OOD; that federation clearly
beats the average local model out of distribution; that the apparent OOD ``win''
of a single small cohort is the covariate-match artifact described above rather
than method skill, and how to detect it; that the one cohort where pooling hurts
on its own distribution is recovered by structural rather than mixture-style
personalisation; and that, at the 30-minute horizon, all methods operate close to
a persistence-dominated ceiling, so the lever for further gains is exogenous
inputs such as insulin bolus rather than larger models.

Taken together, the system shows that federated glucose forecasting can be run,
not just simulated, across institutions and borders, and the benchmark shows
which federated strategy to run and how to evaluate it honestly.
